# Parametric Interpolation of the DLR Turbulent Jet Diffusion Flame using HOSVD + GPR

**Tags:** Combustion · Machine Learning · Reduced-Order Modelling

---

Fast predictions of thermochemical variables is one of the necessary steps in making a functional digital twin for reactive flowsystems. High fidelity CFD simulations are at the core of the study and design of combustion systems, but are often too slow and expensive to generate a full spectrum of the behaviour of the system for a wide spectrum of operating conditions.

This page shows a method developed by out group able to learn from a handful of high fidelity simulation and predict the behaviour of the system for unseen conditions.
The framework is to use Higher Order Singular Value Decomposition to reduce the dimensionality of the simulations and Gaussian Process Regression to interpolate the behaviour for new combinations of operating parameters. 


---

## Dataset

The test case is the DLR turbulent jet diffusion flame, a well-established benchmark for non-premixed combustion. A methane–hydrogen blend is injected through a central jet into a coaxial air stream, simulated with RANS k-ε and the EDC model under an axial symmetry assumption. Numerical results show good agreement with published experimental data.

A total of 100 CFD cases were generated by varying two operating parameters:

| Parameter | Range | Steps |
|---|---|---|
| Reynolds number (Re) | 11,000 – 20,000 | 10 |
| H₂ mass fraction (X_H₂) | 4% – 22% | 10 |

Each simulation stores velocity, temperature, pressure, and species mass fractions (CH₄, O₂, CO, CO₂, H₂O) organised in a five-dimensional tensor indexed by Re, X_H₂, variable, and two spatial coordinates.

## Method

The framework works in two stages: **HOSVD** compresses the training data into a compact tensor decomposition, and **GPR** interpolates the decomposition coefficients for new operating conditions.

### 1 · Higher-Order Singular Value Decomposition (HOSVD)

HOSVD generalises matrix SVD to tensor data. Rather than flattening the dataset into a matrix, it extracts dominant patterns along each dimension simultaneously, producing a core tensor and a set of orthogonal factor matrices — one per dimension. The factor matrices along the parameter axes encode how the flow fields vary independently with Re and X_H₂.

The leading spatial modes extracted for the temperature field are shown below.

![HOSVD spatial modes for temperature](/assets/img/Tutorial/Combustion/hosvd_gpr/modes_hosvd_temperature.png)

### 2 · Gaussian Process Regression (GPR)

A key advantage of HOSVD's separable structure is that the two parameters can be treated with two independent 1D GPR models — one along the Re axis, one along X_H₂. GPR is a non-parametric Bayesian method: it returns a full posterior distribution over predictions, providing both a mean estimate and a spatially resolved uncertainty at no extra cost.

For a new operating condition, the interpolated factor vectors are contracted with the core tensor and the result is rescaled to physical units.

## Results

The framework was evaluated on a held-out case at Re = 13,000, X_H₂ = 8%. The plot below shows the GPR fit along the Re axis for the first four HOSVD modes — black dots are training coefficients, the gold diamond is the true unseen coefficient, and the red marker is the GPR prediction with its ±2σ uncertainty band.

![GPR interpolation of HOSVD coefficients along the Re axis](/assets/img/Tutorial/Combustion/hosvd_gpr/gpr_interpolation_Re_axis.png)

The reconstructed flow field achieves sub-2% relative L₂ error across all variables, with CO showing the highest error due to its sharper spatial gradients.
